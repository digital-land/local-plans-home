Guidance

What to consider when engaging on your local plan

Stages to go through when preparing to engage with people on your local plan.

From:
Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities

Details

Outline your consultation approach

There are many opportunities to outline your consultation approach early in the local plans process. Most notably, this can include developing an engagement plan, which will act as your guide.

When setting out your approach, you should consider:

  • who you want to consult
  • what specific feedback you want
  • how that feedback will help shape your plan
  • how consultation will benefit the community

This final point is arguably the most important. All efforts should be made to show how consultation will shape the local plan.

Defining your approach early also means you’re able to start engagement early. This can give you the best chance to speak to, get feedback from, and shape your plan around your consultees.

Contact statutory consultees

It’s a good idea to personalise your approach to contacting statutory consultees. Notifying them individually allows you to share evidence that’s more relevant to them.

Examples include links and documents to key information based on their expertise. This will save them time they would otherwise spend looking for the evidence. You can also make sure nothing is missed.

Choose the right feedback formats

Considering how you invite and collate comments is important. It makes sure the consultation is openly accessible, and that the analysis of responses is as efficient as possible.

Methods of flexible feedback can include:

  • online surveys
  • feedback forms
  • interactive maps and data
  • in-person workshops

As when contacting statutory consultees, the goal is to get feedback that matters and will make a difference to your plan.

Prepare your consultation report

Once you’ve finished consultation, you should document your approach in a consultation report.

It should set out:

  • the bodies and people invited to comment
  • a summary of their responses
  • key issues/themes you identified through analysis
  • your responses to these

This should include reference to how you’ll change the local plan and where you may need any further evidence base work.

You should also include all the methods that you used to make people aware of the consultation.

Consider future needs

Depending on what comes out of your consultations, you may need to do further work. You could target different audiences, or focus on wider engagement or evidence base collection. Whatever the approach, you should consider it if it will make the local plan better.

For example, you might include workshops with:

  • internal officers
  • statutory consultees
  • key stakeholders, including locally constituted community groups and designated neighbourhood plan forums

Where possible, you should look to resolve any outstanding issues. At this stage, these will likely involve infrastructure providers and strategic site promoters/major housebuilders.
