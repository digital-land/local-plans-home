A more transparent call for sites through community workshops

How Ashford Borough Council was able to balance consultation and evidence gathering.

From:
Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities
Published
20 March 2024
Last updated
18 December 2023 — See all updates

Ashford Borough Council faced what they called ‘a typical problem’ when creating their local plan; delivering housing growth while maintaining environmental/countryside protection.

They needed to understand the concerns of the local community while progressing with housing allocations. This is how they approached the challenge.
Caption

The approach

  • Transparent conversations during engagement
  • Call for sites run after building a shared consensus 
  • Clear articulation of decisions for examination

Creating space for transparent conversations

Our starting point was to get out into the community to have a grown-up conversation with residents and parish councillors about the challenges we faced. 

You need to be 100 percent transparent in the way in which you explain the issues to your community. Ashford haven’t used ‘issues and options’ style documents as a rule, although there are instances in which they can be helpful to build consensus around approaches. 

We started with ‘Plan Ashford’, which was really a series of conversations to understand what the issues were.

Building a shared consensus before a milestone

We then did a ‘call for sites’ exercise and a series of exhibitions jointly with the parish councils. 

We presented the available sites on a map without indicating their suitability. We used this as a basis for discussions with communities on what their thoughts were on these sites – the issues and opportunities. 

Understanding what your plan is seeking to achieve is key. In terms of developing the strategy, you need a clear narrative – and that narrative needs to be underpinned by evidence. Don’t start from sites to develop the strategy as you don’t always meet in the middle.

Being able to show decisions during assessment

Being able to point the inspector to evidence and articulate why you have made certain choices – and how you have exercised your judgement – is key. 

This can mean that you have allocated the ‘best site’ based on a strategy decision rather than consideration of the site ‘scores’ in isolation. But you have to be able to justify and explain this. 

Being able to point an inspector to the relevant section of an evidence document whether in statements or during hearings inspires confidence.

In summary

Simon Cole, Head of Planning Policy, said:cc

The strength of a local plan is based on two pillars: consultation and evidence gathering. You need to approach these proportionately and get the balance right between them.
