Guidance

Running a call for sites

Learn where and when to publicise your call for sites to potential respondents, including what information you should collect on each site.

From:
Ministry of Housing, Communities & Local Government
Published
23 August 2024

A call for sites is the most effective way of finding other sites for potential development. It asks developers, landowners, public bodies and the general public to propose land they think is right for development. These can then be assessed, with the most appropriate allocated during the local plan process.

It’s also a good chance to keep local residents informed of your local plan timetable. This is key to keeping them informed of the progress you are making.

Anyone can propose sites for development, even if they do not own the land they’re proposing.

Learn more about Stage 2: Categorising and assessing sites, Stage 3: Arriving at draft site allocations, or Stage 4: Confirming sites and recording your decisions

Aim of this section

  • how to effectively publicise a call for sites
  • understand the different ways and channels to reach potential respondents
  • what information to include in your call for sites

Outputs of this section

  • sites put forward for development
  • detailed information about each site
  • (optional) a published list of potential sites

Who to target with a call for sites

You should issue the call for sites to as wide an audience as possible, including:

  • developers
  • landowners
  • public bodies
  • the general public
  • estate agents
  • local residents
  • local councillors
  • landowners
  • planning consultants
  • house builders
  • businesses and interest groups
  • parish and town councils
  • neighbourhood planning forums
  • other departments from your LPA itself

Remember - anyone can propose land for development, so include whoever you need to give you a complete audit of your options.

When publicising the call for sites, share as much information about your local plan as possible. This will give respondents useful context and make it more likely they suggest appropriate sites.

This should include:

  • an early vision of the vision for your area, to be included in the draft local plan
  • an initial assessment of housing and need
  • a timetable for the plan

When and how to publicise your call for sites

You can run a call for sites in a way that suits you best. This could be open-ended throughout the process, periodically open at certain times, or open for a defined number of weeks. The approach you take will determine the number of sites that are likely to be put forward.

You can keep your call open for the length of the site identification and selection process. This might increase the number of responses, but make assessment more difficult if sites are still being proposed throughout. The approach you take to timings will depend on the desired outcome for your local authority.

Publicise your call for sites where people are most likely to see it. It will increase your chances of getting more respondents, and make the process more transparent.

Examples of good places to publicise include:

  • your local council website
  • local bulletins
  • public meetings
  • direct emails to interested people
  • media channels, for example, the council magazine

What information to ask for in a call for sites

It’s essential to be clear about what you want from respondents.

It helps to standardise the information you receive and makes categorising and assessment easier. It also ensures the process is transparent and fair, as responders do not share more than they need to if expectations are clear.

Where possible, use an online form to collect the information you need. It will help make it more likely that respondents share on;y what you’ve asked for, and in a consistent format. This will save time when it comes to reviewing responses.

There is a minimum level of site data you should collect. It should give you enough detail to make an informed assessment. As well as this, you have the flexibility to ask any locally relevant questions that might help the process.

Sometimes, not everyone will be able to share all of this information. You can decide whether what they provide is enough to work with. You may also be able to combine it with extra information you collect yourself.

Reviewing and collecting extra information

When advertising your call for sites, you should be clear that any information provided that’s not relevant at this stage may not be used. You can reserve the right to not review it.

Equally, if you have a large number of sites and feel extra information would be useful, you can request it at a later date.

You may also need to collect extra information independently from a call for sites to help with assessment.

This may include:

  • greenfield and brownfield land registers
  • planning applications
  • previous land availability assessments 
  • the national planning constraints database

You can decide if this is needed once you have initially sifted your sites.

Contacting respondents

You should tell anyone who submits information when you:

  • have received their submission
  • will assess their site
  • will be in contact if you need more information

You should also confirm that you will hold their data in accordance with data protection principles. Otherwise, there’s no need to contact respondents unless you need to clarify anything with them.

Examples of things you may need to clarify include:

  • checking general facts and information they’ve given you
  • development progress for sites with planning permission
  • understanding the type and scale of development for proposed sites
  • more detail on deliverability and how any barriers might be overcome

Publishing your sites

Once you’ve identified all the sites you need, it’s up to you whether you publish the list at this stage or not. There is no requirement to do so, but it could help make the process more transparent.

Publishing gives residents the chance to see the proposed sites and provide more information, if available. It also helps them feel more connected to the local plan and allows them to track the identification and selection process more closely.

If you decide to publish, we recommend including a detailed or interactive map for increased clarity. It is also essential to point out that these sites have not been assessed or guaranteed to be allocated at this stage.

A detailed map should include the sites’:

  • name
  • location
  • suggested land use

Next steps

Once you have finished identifying your sites and gathering the information you need, you should start Stage 2: categorising and assessing sites.
