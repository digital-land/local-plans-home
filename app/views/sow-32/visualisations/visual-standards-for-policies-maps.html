Local plans must include policies maps

Local plans must include a policies map, which shows how all of a local planning authority’s (LPA’s) development policies apply to different geographical areas.

Recommended visual standards

This page sets out visual standards that we recommend LPAs follow when creating policies maps. It’s aimed at those creating the maps - which may be planners, GIS specialists or people working for mapping software companies.

These standards are not required - they are recommendations. We’ve created them through research and testing with LPAs and property technology suppliers.

If all LPAs follow a consistent, tested format for policies maps, this will mean:

  • maps are quicker and easier to produce
  • maps are easier to understand and are more accessible for people viewing them
  • it’s easier to look across maps from different authorities - for example, for developers looking to develop in a range of areas across the country, or for members of the public who live on the border of two authorities
  • there’s less duplication of effort by LPAs each creating their own style and format

These standards may be mandated in future, as part of planning reforms. We’ve created this guidance now so you can see the direction of travel. We’ll update this page when we have more information about how they’ll apply in future.

Basemaps

Choose from the 2 options below for your basemap. This is the map that you show all your policy information on.

These basemap styles are generally easiest for your users to see your policies when they’re layered on top, due to their visual simplicity and monochrome colour schemes.

Option 1: greyscale street view with street names

A greyscale street view is a familiar view to most users. The street names help users understand and navigate the map. Generally speaking, it’s clear to see coloured layers added on top of the map.

Option 2: mastermap mono

Removes the street names and shows a more detailed view. Generally speaking, it’s clear to see coloured layers added on top of the map.

Additional option: aerial photography

This can bring the map to life and help users understand the detail of the landscape.

If you offer aerial photography, it should be an additional option to the greyscale street view view or mastermap mono - not a replacement. This is because it can be harder to see layers on top, due to the level of detail and different colours.

There are other issues to be aware of when using aerial photography:

  • it’s not suitable for physical maps because of the amount of ink you need to use when printing and photocopying it
  • it’s not always up-to-date

Policy categories

We recommend grouping your policies into 3 different categories, and presenting each category differently on the policies map.

Below are the categories to use and how to show each one.

If you can, you should include the description as well as the category name so that everyone can understand what the categories mean.

These categories were clear and accessible to the members of the public who tested them. Other options which included more technical terms like ‘spatial’ were not as clear.

Site allocations

Description: land identified and designated for development with a particular use, for example, housing and employment.

How to show them: Use diagonal hatching with a border width of 2 pixels to show site allocations on the map.

Broader area-based policies

Description: policies based around a topic or geographical area, such as a part of the city or district, for example, tourism or student housing policies.

How to show them: Use a colour fill at 80% transparency with a border width of 2 pixels to show broader area-based policies on the map.

Authority-wide policies

Description: policies that apply to the whole plan area, for example, affordable housing and housing mix policies, or they may be topic-based.

How to show them: You do not need to illustrate these policies visually. As they cover the whole map area, showing them visually makes the map too busy and confusing.

However, you do still need to include them on the map. You can choose how to do this. An example might be a text box that includes a list of all authority-wide policies, and an explanation that they apply to the whole area.

Policy themes

Group your policies into the following 8 themes, and allocate the relevant colour to each theme.

These themes have been tested with planners and members of the public to make sure they are clear and well-understood.

Below we’ve set out the codes for each theme, as well as some examples of which types of policies you could put into each theme.

We’ve provided the examples to help you understand what we mean by each theme, and to help you map your policies to them. You do not need to have these policies or use these exact names - you’re free to call your policies whatever you like.

Housing

Colours:

  • Hex code #c44200
  • RGB (196, 66, 0)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Housing density and mix
  • Housing need
  • Affordable housing

Economy

Colours:

  • Hex code #330165
  • RGB (51, 1, 101)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Mixed-use development
  • Retaining land for employment uses
  • Employment land supply
  • Rural development sites

Major change

Colours:

  • Hex code #007A7A
  • RGB (0, 122, 122)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Growth zones or areas of opportunity
  • Strategic regeneration areas
  • New settlements or major urban extensions

Heritage and build design

Colours:

  • Hex code #CD2380
  • RGB (205, 35, 128)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Historic environment
  • Design
  • Tall buildings

Natural environment

Colours:

  • Hex code #217E01
  • RGB (33, 126, 1)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Flood risk
  • Biodiversity
  • Green belt or open space

Culture, leisure and tourism

Colours:

  • Hex code #6C003B
  • RGB (108, 0, 59)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Culture, tourism and sport facilities

Community services and wellbeing

Colours:

  • Hex code #2A72B2
  • RGB (42, 114, 178)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Health and wellbeing
  • Community facilities and assets
  • Recreational spaces

Transport and infrastructure

Colours:

  • Hex code #001B4D
  • RGB (0, 27, 77)

Examples of policies to group under this theme:

  • Sustainable travel
  • Transport assessments
  • Blue and green infrastructure
  • Climate change and renewable energy
  • Minerals and waste

Colour accessibility

Each colour meets a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio when overlaid on the grayscale basemap above with no transparency applied (for example, the borders of all areas). This means they contrast well enough with the background map to be distinguishable. This is the ratio recommended by the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines.

The colours chosen also meet the 3:1 ratio when placed alongside each other in pairs. The colours get less accessible when you place multiple colours alongside each other or overlapping though.

Considerations when showing all policies at once

You must have a version of the policies map that shows all your policies at once.

However, showing all policies on the map at once is often visually overwhelming for users and means it’s hard to focus on the areas they’re most interested in.

Ideally, you would let users filter on an interactive map. Or, you could have additional versions of the map that show different categories.

Have alternative formats available

Maps are hard to make fully accessible for everyone. This means you should try to make sure that there are other ways people can interact with the information that your policies map shows.

The policies map should not be the only way that people can learn about your policies - it should illustrate policy information which is also set out in your local plan.

Having the maps themselves available in multiple formats will help people with disabilities and other access needs get the information they’re entitled to.

For example, if you use an interactive map, not everyone will be able to use it. You must not exclude anyone because of this. You could also make available:

  • a static version of the map for those who cannot use interactive online software
  • a way of seeing the map for those who cannot access the internet independently

If you’re using a supplier to create your map, they may have the ability to present the data included in the map in a different format. For example, in ESRI you can view the map data in a table format instead.
