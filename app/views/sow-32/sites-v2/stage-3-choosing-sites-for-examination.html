Choosing sites for examination

Taking site allocations you have drafted and confirming which you wish to take forward into examination.

From:
Ministry of Housing, Communities & Local Government
Published
23 August 2013

Contents

  1. Testing your preferred candidate sites
  2. Outputs at the end of drafting site allocations
  3. Next steps

This is where you identify preferred candidate sites for your local plan. They should be sites that best meet your plan’s needs and align with your spatial strategy and vision. You will need to build in sufficient time between engagement / consultations on the draft site allocations and examination to be able to properly consider responses.

You should follow the process for both housing and employment sites at the same time. This helps ensure all sites are used most effectively.

Aim of this section

  • test your draft sites, including any alternatives
  • understand the outputs you need by the end of the process

Outputs of this section

  • a list of confirmed sites for examination
  • your rationale for each selected site

Testing your preferred candidate sites

You should test your candidate sites on a settlement or sub-area scale.

Test your sites by recording:

  • why it is appropriate - including how it meets your local need and spatial strategy
  • how they meet your ‘best fit’ approach
  • how achievable each site is - including impact on local services, like schools or utilities

As well as your preferred candidate sites, you should test any available alternatives. This will allow for growth at the settlement or sub-area. You should consider whether they will provide a pipeline of deliverable and developable sites.

Considering alternative sites will also feed into your plan's Strategic Environmental Assessment. You can follow separate guidance for more detail on this.

Outputs at the end of drafting site allocations

As referenced throughout the process, it’s essential to include clear rationale about the decisions you make throughout your site selection process. It gives your stakeholders clarity on how you arrived at your sites, and why they are suitable.

It's also easier to report on these decisions if you’ve documented them effectively.

You need to report on:

  • sites you have selected for your local plan
  • sites you have not selected
  • your justification for each site

Planning Inspectors are looking for a clear narrative of why you have chosen your sites, and whether those decisions changed throughout your assessments. It is important to include the scores you gave for each site at Stage 2 (‘policy-off’) as well as Stage 3 (‘policy-on’).

Your justifications must include why each site is or is not suitable for your plan.

You should also give reasons for how the site meets the local need and spatial strategy. Justifications should be clear but succinct, at no longer than 3-4 sentences for each site.

Describing your methodology

It’s essential you explain the methodology you decided to use when it comes to examination. This is key to rationalising your decisions on specific sites.

You should detail your approach, including:

  • an overview of the methodology
  • how you applied this to different site types
  • how you considered the factors listed above
  • how it gave you the best possible outcome

You must specifically reference why your methodology was most appropriate for your local context. This is a significant consideration during examination.

For example, where you’ve previously assessed a site as amber, but have since upgraded it based on more information. In this case, you should explain this new information, and why it led you to re-categorise the site.

Formatting your decisions

It is strongly recommended that you set your decisions out in a summary table format. This is to create clarity at examination and go alongside any detailed documentation from your assessments at Stages 2 and 3.

You may be working from, and producing, several documents when assessing your sites in Stages 2 and 3. The main need from a Planning Inspector is to follow the narrative of each site between these documents. A summary table helps create this clarity.

Planning Inspectors are looking for the following detail:

  • site name or reference
  • site location
  • how much you can build on each site
  • the time it will take to build on each site
  • individual RAG scores for suitability, availability and achievability
  • overall RAG score
  • justifications for overall score, including detail on how this may have changed between Stages 2 and 3
  • indication or ordering of the sites to show which ones you have deemed unsuitable, and which ones are your draft allocations

Using this standardised approach gives you a better chance of your plan being found sound at examination. It allows examiners to extract information quickly without the need for excessive detail. It is also quick and easy to format and edit if needed.

Next steps

By finishing this section, you’ve completed all the work needed for identifying and selecting sites.

Your chosen sites will form part of your wider local plan, which will be reviewed at examination once ready.
