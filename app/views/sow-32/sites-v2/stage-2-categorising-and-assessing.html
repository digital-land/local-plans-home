Categorising sites

A guide to categorising and assessing sites, including suggested methodologies and information to record for examination.

From:
Ministry of Housing, Communities & Local Government
Published
23 August 2013

Once you’ve identified all viable sites, you need to categorise them before you can start assessment.

If respondents have followed the steps outlined in stage 1, they will have categorised their sites already. It’s important you review the categories, however. You’ll also need to categorise any sites you identified from existing allocations or planning applications.

Categorising helps you understand if you need to ask for more information. This could be anything that helps you assess and allocate your sites. This will depend on what you can realistically build on each site, and your spatial strategy.

Learn more about Stage 1: Identifying Sites, or Stage 3: Arriving at draft site allocations

How to categorise sites

You should categorise sites by the proposed type and scale of development.

The three main categories you should categorise into are:

  • Greenfield-led housing sites
  • Brownfield-led housing sites
  • Employment-led sites

You should also consider traveller sites as part of your duty to all groups with protected characteristics.

As will all other stages, you’re encouraged to use your own judgement on categorisation when making decisions. You will have to explain your rationale during the examination process.

What is the main purpose for the development?

This is to decide whether a site is employment or housing-led.

Where a site is mixed-use, you should categorise based on its main use. So, if a site is predominantly for housing but will have some employment uses, it should be categorised as housing-led. You can be flexible on mixed use depending on the local context and your site needs.

If a site is employment-led, you do not need to categorise by greenfield or brownfield. Development options for these sites focus on the type of proposed employment rather than location.

Is it a greenfield or brownfield site?

If more than half of development is going to happen on previously undeveloped land, this means it is greenfield-led. Brownfield-led developments occur predominantly on land that has been developed before.

Remember, you do not need to categorise at this level for employment-led sites.

Example categories and development types

Once you’ve categorised your sites, it’s useful to break them down further into development types. This gives you a better view of what each potential site offers, and helps make sure you’re meeting all needs.

You should define these categories yourself based on your judgement and local circumstances. There should be a strong rationale behind your categories, which you can explain at evaluation.

Below is an example of some development categories. Most are flexible, and can be adapted to your needs. Where categories are fixed, this is to give balance and ensure a strong evidence base.

Categories have been suggested based on:

  • likely lead-in times for different-sized sites
  • different evidence requirements to support each site
  • development scale vs. likely impact

Estimating development potential

Development potential essentially means how many developments a site can hold.

Potential varies between different site categories, and will depend on things like site density and any constraints on the site.

Housing

This potential depends on how densely developments will be built - density assumptions - and other factors like call for sites data, size constraints and other constraints.

For all sites, you should make sure it makes the most efficient use of the land in line with national policy.

When considering this for newer , you should think about:

  • net developable area, depending on site type and constraints. You could apply gross-to-net ratios, reflecting that a greater proportion of land is likely to be needed for on-site infrastructure for larger sites
  • indicative density multipliers - for example, size and location - based on existing densities, or consideration of what an appropriate density could be in the future
  • any relevant adopted or emerging policies - for example, national space / car parking standards, design policies, green infrastructure and biodiversity net gain requirements
  • commercial viability - the types of development that are likely to be viable in the location
  • site-specific opportunity - for example, very accessible, urban locations suitable for higher density proposals that meet your local needs

Employment

It’s likely that most employment sites will be able to support multiple categories of development. Therefore, the aim is to identify all potential employment development categories.

This will inform future economic studies around the scale and employment type needed. These can then be more easily compared to existing supply.

You can use indicative plot ratios to estimate potential based on the type of expected employment floorspace. You can also find employment development market intelligence to inform the ratio of buildings to land.

This is useful if you do not have site-specific evidence from site promoters.

Sites in multiple categories

For both housing and employment, having sites in more than one development category is acceptable. But you should be careful not to double count a site and its development potential during this stage.

You should decide on the most appropriate category for these sites by the end of stage 3.

Re-categorising your sites

Changing your site categories based on new information is normal and acceptable. This should be done before stage 3 of the process.

For example, a developer’s proposal during a call for sites might not represent the most efficient use of land, or include all constraints. If they evolve their understanding, you should be comfortable re-categorising based on this.

Next steps

Once you have finished categorising, you’re ready to start site assessment.
