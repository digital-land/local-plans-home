Why it’s useful to categorise sites

Categorising your sites will help inform your spatial strategy, as this will give you a good view of what you can build based on the available sites. 

It will also highlight what other types of evidence you need to be able to assess and allocate your sites. This will differ depending on your area. 

How you could categorise sites

Categorising sites involves different ways to assess the type of land on a site and what it’s possible to build there.

You could use this initial step to split your sites into:

  • greenfield-led housing sites
  • brownfield-led housing sites
  • employment-led sites

This could be useful to do at the start, but is something you may do at another point in your assessment. 

Greenfield-led sites are sites where the leading development is on land not previously built on. 

If your site is mixed-use, it would be considered a housing-led site if housing is the main driver for the development with some supporting employment uses. Employment sites are sites where the main use is employment, with some other uses, such as co-located housing or retail. If you have a site that you’re using for housing and employment that aren’t interlinked, you should split the site and submit as two separate sites.

Deciding what you can build on your sites

To be able to give your sites a category, you need to decide what you’re able to build on the site. There are different ways to do this depending on whether it’s a housing site or an employment site. 

Categorising housing sites

To decide if you can build housing on a site, you should look at:

  • where the site is
  • the size of the site
  • how much you can build on the site
  • information submitted at the call for sites
  • if there has already been a planning application on the site
  • how much of the site can be used for development
  • any relevant emerging policies
  • your vision or emerging strategy

Categorising employment sites

To decide if you can use a site for employment, you should look at all the different types of employment you could have on the site, for example, offices or industrial buildings.

Examples of categories

Greenfield-led sites:

G1 - 10-49 homes - these are smaller developments, more likely to be built out by SMEs with lower evidential threshold.

G2 - 50-499 homes - these are medium sized developments that at the top end may have site specific infrastructure.

G3 - 500+ homes - these are sites where development of a strategic scale means a lead-in time of at least circa 5-years. These sites are more likely to contain site specific infrastructure and will need more evidence to allocate.

G4 - New Settlement or Garden Village/Towns - these are a form of development that needs more significant levels of evidence to allocate; potentially with cross border collaboration.

These are examples of potential categories you could have, however this will depend on your local area. You may consider sites with different scales because of the availability of sites you have. You can categorise your sites in a way that works for your circumstances. You should limit the amount of categories you create to the minimum you need. This is because you’ll need more evidence for certain types of categories.

Recording information during your assessment

As you go through and assess your sites, you need to record what you do and keep this information in a file for each site. For each site you assess, you should record information including:

  • site name or reference
  • site land use (both existing and proposed)
  • site location and size (preferably on a map)
  • site constraints and other considerations
  • site scoring
  • description of why you scored the site the way you did
  • why you have or haven’t taken the site through to the next stage
  • why you made these decisions - reasons why you have or haven’t allocated sites
  • any factors specific to your local area that affect these decisions

You should be as open as possible in your records, as this is what the Planning Inspectorate can use to help them check that your judgement is sound. Include as much detail as you can about why you chose or discarded your sites. This allows inspectors to see the decisions you’ve made, and also understand the detail around them. You can change your initial conclusions about a site later, but should record why you’ve done this.

Starting your assessment and initial sifting

Once you’ve identified and categorised your sites, you could carry out an initial sift of your sites. You should look at things that could be major constraints. These are constraints that mean it might not be suitable land to build on. 

If any of your sites are affected by these constraints, you should ideally discard those sites first, as they are the hardest to mitigate. This will be different depending on the area and some of these constraints might not stop you from taking your sites forward into a detailed assessment. You should use your judgement based on your local area and the sites you have available. 

There are a number of national constraints that could affect your ability to build on sites, which includes:

  • Ramsar/Special Protection Area/Special Areas of Conservation
  • Sites of Special Scientific Interest (SSSIs)
  • Scheduled Monuments
  • Ancient Woodlands (primarily)
  • Flood Zones 3a and 3b
  • Public Safety Zones (i.e. airports)
  • Detailed Emergency Planning Zones
  • Health & Safety Executive Consultation Zones
  • Village Green / Common Land

You should use your judgement based on your local area and the sites you have available to decide if these constraints mean you cannot develop on the site. If you find any of your sites have these constraints, but you can mitigate the risks, you should record this and take the sites forward for detailed assessment.

The next step is to assess each site’s suitability, availability and achievability. This includes whether it’s likely to be viable. This will help you decide if a site might be deliverable or developable. 

Assessing the suitability of sites

The suitability of sites is influenced by:

  • national planning policy
  • local planning policy
  • physical constraints
  • impact of development and mitigation

Constraints to consider

At this stage, you should consider:

  • existing local and national policy constraints
  • local policy choices made through the plan, such as green belt
    highway access
  • neighbouring amenities
  • sustainability and accessibility

Other constraints needing review and mitigation

There are other constraints that you’ll need to consider. These include:

  • areas of outstanding national beauty
  • local green space
  • areas at risk of flooding or coastal change
  • local nature sites
  • national parks
  • minerals policy
  • listed buildings
  • waste policies
  • groundwater source protection
  • heritage designations
  • green belt
  • local wildlife sites
  • coastal protection belt
  • tree preservation orders
  • local biodiversity considerations
  • best and most versatile agricultural land
  • aviation constraints
  • relevant buffer zones
  • public rights of way
  • contamination

These should still be carefully considered but mitigation may be easier in these cases.

Using our approach to score your sites

There are lots of options for how you might score your sites to help you decide which you take forward to the next stage and which you discard. You should avoid using a complex, weighted approach.

Our preferred approach is the Red, Amber, Green (RAG) approach. This uses a simple system to score sites.

The Red, Amber, Green (RAG) approach

The Red, Amber, Green (RAG) approach is where you’d give each site a colour coded scoring depending on how suitable it is. We’ve created some examples to show how this could work.

Example 1 - Conservation area RAG scoring

You need to decide whether developing in a conservation area would cause substantial harm.
 
Red - likely substantial harm
Amber - mitigation is possible
Green - no substantial harm would be caused, any minor harm mitigated

At the end of this suitability assessment, you should look at whether part of a site could be used with less constraints.

Example 2 - Highway access RAG rating

You need to assess whether a site has suitable access.

Red - not safely achievable
Amber - constrained access
Green - suitable existing access or new access is achievable

This approach should help you create your categories and add RAG scoring to each site based on the constraints it has. Once you’ve done this, you’ll need to balance out the constraints with mitigation for each site. This will help you decide which sites you take forward to draft allocation. You can use your own process for this based on your local context.

Assessing the availability of sites

You should have information from the call for sites to check the availability of a site. It should be considered available if the landowner or promoter has said they intend to build on the site or sell it for development, and there are no legal constraints. 

Sites that might be considered unavailable include sites with:

  • multiple owners with no agreements
  • ransom strips
  • tenancies
  • covenants

You should include timescales for availability in your documentation, as well as where site availability is unknown. 

Assessing the achievability of sites

The achievability of a site is assessed based on if it can be built on at a particular point in time and if it is likely to be viable. You should carry out an initial viability study reviewing the sites at a high-level using typologies or a sample of sites. This will help highlight if your preferred spatial strategy is achievable.

You should consider any sites with a typology that is unviable to be potentially unachievable at this stage. A whole plan viability study at a later stage will check the viability of specific sites and any policy requirements your plan might need to consider.

When you’ve completed your detailed assessment, you should have all of the information you need about your sites documented.

This should include:

  • which sites are suitable, available and achievable
  • any mitigation measures
  • if a site is or isn’t viable
  • when you can expect to build on the land
  • anywhere that you’ve recategorised a site based on this information

The Planning Inspectorate will use this information to help determine if your allocations are sound.

What to do if you don’t have enough sites

If you do not have enough sites at the end of your detailed assessment, you could try:

  • reconsidering any sites you discarded during the assessment process
  • doing another call for sites 
  • documenting why you don’t have enough sites and what you did to try to find more sites

There is separate guidance on this.

What you should have at the end of your assessment

When you have done your detailed assessment, you should have:

  • a list of sites you’ve identified
  • a map of the site locations
  • how much you can build on each site
  • the time it will take to build on each site
  • barriers to overcome

You’ll also need a file or document which includes:

  • any sites you discarded
  • the sites you’ve chosen for potential development
  • any sites you discarded at later points
  • evidence and justification of why you discarded each site
  • evidence and justification of why you took the sites forward that you chose for allocation
