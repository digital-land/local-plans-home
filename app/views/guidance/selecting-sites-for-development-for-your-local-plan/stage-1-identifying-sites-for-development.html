How to identify sites

You should actively identify sites by putting out a call for sites. This is a way to ask developers, landowners, public bodies and members of the public if they have land that you can use. They can then put their land forward for you to assess for potential development. 

You should also look at new and existing sites that you might have seen before in:
local or neighbourhood plans

  • the brownfield land register
  • planning applications
  • pre-application submissions - in agreement with developers
  • any current or previous land availability assessments
  • land owned by the local authority and other public bodies in consultation
  • new or previous capacity or intensification assessments
  • sites previously identified or allocated that could be improved, intensified, or changed

You should try to gather all potential sites at this stage. This will form your list of potential sites to take forward into the assessment stage of the process. 

The types of sites

This framework covers sites for housing and employment (including mixed use sites). 

Housing sites are where you might have:

  • market housing for sale or rent (including build to rent and co-living)
  • affordable housing
  • specialist elderly housing
  • self-build or custom-build housing
  • student accommodation

Employment sites are where you might have:

  • offices, leisure and retail
  • research and development and knowledge
  • industrial buildings
  • logistics
  • data centres
  • employment-led mixed use

Who to consult when identifying sites

Your call for sites should be aimed at:

  • landowners
  • agents
  • developers
  • local residents
  • local councillors
  • businesses and interest groups
  • parish and town councils
  • other departments from your local planning authority itself 
  • other public bodies with land  
    You should identify and contact known landowners or developers behind key local sites.

How and when to run a call for sites

Your call for sites should be with as wide an audience as possible. You could share some information about your plan to help people to understand your needs, including:

  • an early vision
  • initial assessment of housing and need
  • a timetable for the plan

You should use a range of different ways to advertise your call for sites to your local community, including:

  • your local council website
  • local bulletins
  • public meetings
  • emails
  • media channels

You can set a clear deadline for submission of at least 8 weeks, but you can also keep your call for sites open if this suits your needs. If you keep your call for sites open, you could get sites being submitted at any point in the process. You might not be able to include any sites that come in late to your emerging plan.

What to ask for in your call for sites

Your call for sites should detail what information you need from developers, landowners and other people or groups who may put sites forward. Creating a form to gather the information is a useful way to make sure you get what you need at this point. Limiting the questions you ask in your form to just what you need at this point will lower the risk of getting too much information that isn’t relevant. You may not get all of this information at this point if the people or organisations submitting don’t have it.

This could include:

  • who the person or organisation is and their contact details
  • how they relate to the site, for example, are they the landowner
  • who owns the site and whether it is under option or promotion agreement
  • where the site is, with an address or co-ordinates and a GIS file
  • type of site
  • previous and current use of the site
  • planning history
  • details of previous site promotion or pre-application discussion
  • proposed use of the site
  • how much can be built
  • known constraints and mitigations
  • when development could start
  • how much existing development is already on a site
  • any other questions relevant to your local planning authority

You should make it clear that you may not use any extra information that is submitted but not needed at this stage.

What information you need to collect yourself

You might need to collect information outside of the call for sites to help you properly assess the site. This could include:

  • greenfield and brownfield land registers
  • planning applications
  • previous land availability assessments 
  • the national planning constraints database

Letting people know where you are in the process

You should tell anyone who submits information when you have:

  • received their submission
  • will assess their site
  • will be in contact if you need more information

What happens next

Once you have finished identifying your sites and gathering the information you need, you should start your assessment. To help you assess the sites, you can categorise them based on the scale and type of development.
