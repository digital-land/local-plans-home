30-month process explained

Understand the timeframes, stages and deliverables involved to prepare, create, and adopt a local plan. 

From:
Ministry of Housing, Communities & Local Government
Published
18 December 2023

Contents

  1. Process overview
  2. 4 months’ notice: Give notice of start of plan-making
  3. 23 months: Plan-making
  4. 6 months: Examination
  5. 1 month: Finalisation and adoption of digital plan

New policy outlines how to create and adopt a local plan in 30 months. It is designed to reduce the amount of time spent by everyone involved in the local plan process.

It also aims to deliver adopted local plans that better meet the needs of those creating them, and those affected by plans in the local community.

You can use this guide to understand:

  • What’s included in each stage of the process
  • What you need to prepare during each stage
  • Where you can learn more about what’s involved in each stage

Process overview

Timescale Stages
4 months’ notice Give notice of start of plan-making
23 months Plan-making
6 months Examination
1 month Finalisation and adoption of digital plan

4 months’ notice: Give notice of start of plan-making

Planning authorities are expected to give notice that they have started plan-making.

Scoping and early participation

Scoping is a crucial early step in plan-making. It happens before the official 30-month process begins. It allows planning authorities to define the plan’s scope, agree the draft vision, and spatial options that will be discussed at the first gateway. 

Unlike the 30-month process, there’s no proposed time limit for this stage.

What you need to deliver:

  • A Project Initiation Document for submission before you move into plan visioning and strategy development (Gateway 1)
  • Include the scope of evidence, match resources, project management, and approach to engagement with members and other stakeholders
  • Prepare and publish a plan timetable
  • Identify your monitoring requirements

Learn more about what you need to start a local plan.

23 months: Plan-making

Planning gets underway. Planners will go back and forth between different stages until they are happy with the evidence they have created.

Plan visioning and strategy development

Plan visioning and strategy development will overlap with evidence gathering and drafting of the plan. To avoid delays, we recommend undertaking tasks in parallel during the first 6 months. 

If your evidence is not subject to public debate or dependent on key decisions, it should be done as quickly as possible.

This stage of the process involves looking ahead of time, and thinking about the long-term goals and aspirations for your community. 

You’ll need to start with a readiness assessment. This will make sure you’ve completed all pre-planning tasks, and you’re set to start the next stage.

Local authorities will need to consider and agree on different spatial options. Once the consultation period ends and feedback is analysed, the council will make a final decision on the preferred spatial option.

You will finish this stage by completing the first gateway assessment. 

What you need to do:

  • Prepare and hold the first public consultation
  • Prepare a vision about the future of the area
  • Confirm evidence requirements
  • Identity and test spatial options and local priorities from the public consultation
  • Complete Gateway 1 

What you need to deliver:

  • Plan vision, mission aims and objectives
  • Submission form for gateway

Gateway 1

Gateways help to validate your plan at important stages of its development. Each has a different focus, designed to give you different feedback when you need it.

Focus:

  • Ensuring the plan sets off in the right direction
  • Supporting early diagnosis of potential issues – legal and procedural requirements and soundness
  • Duration of 4 weeks (up to 6 by exception)

Learn more about what you need to create a vision and strategy for your local plan.

Evidence gathering and drafting the plan

This is a resource heavy period. To help manage the workload, local authorities should start this task in parallel with plan visioning, or during the scoping stage.

Evidence can be split by type:

  • Whether it’s a national or local requirement
  • Whether it affects the project schedule — generic evidence can be prepared at any stage, from scoping to final drafting of the plan. Other evidence can only be prepared once the spatial strategy has been agreed.

What you need to do:

  • Produce evidence to inform, explain, and demonstrate soundness of the plan
  • Engage continuously with members to ensure sign off

What you need to deliver:

  • List of evidence documents
  • Full set of evidence 
  • A draft local plan

Gateway 2

Focus:

  • Supporting early resolution of potential soundness issues, where possible
  • Ensuring legal and procedural compliance
  • Monitoring and tracking progress
  • Duration of 4 weeks (up to 6 by exception)

Learn more about selecting sites.

Engagement, proposition changes, submission

This intensive period, lasting 4 months, is crucial for securing council approval for your local plan before submission for examination.  

What you need to do:

  • Hold a second public consultation with the community, statutory bodies and other relevant stakeholders
  • Analyse the feedback and make changes prior to submission
  • Finalise all requirements for Gateway 3

Gateway 3

Focus:

  • Checking the plan is ready to proceed to examination
  • Ensuring legal and procedural compliance
  • Monitoring and tracking progress
  • Duration of 4 weeks (up to 6 by exception)

6 months: Examination

Plan-making enters the examination stage. 

Examination

Once the Local Planning Authority has finished preparing and consulting on a local plan it must be submitted to the Secretary of State who will appoint an Inspector to carry out an independent examination. This process is dealt with by the Planning Inspectorate.

The examination will assess whether the plan has been prepared in accordance with legal and procedural requirements and if it is sound.

Those who submitted representations will have the right to present their views to the inspector.

1 month: Finalisation and adoption of digital plan

You are expected to finish, adopt, and start monitoring your local plan.

Finalisation and adoption of digital plan

The purpose of authority monitoring is to review progress against local plan policy objectives and targets, as well as other key planning matters.

There are two types of monitoring.

Annual Monitoring: 

  • Fixed, consistent date monitoring.
  • Monitored against a short list of nationally prescribed metrics to assess implementation of key policies.

Detailed Monitoring Return (start in year 4):

  • Consists of a detailed return to inform updates to the plan which will begin at year 5 after adoption.
  • Considers where policies are no longer relevant and informs the upcoming update.​
